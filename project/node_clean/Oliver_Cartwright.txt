<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/wiki/Oliver_Cartwright?veaction=edit&amp;section=1" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/wiki/Oliver_Cartwright?action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology"><svg class="wds-icon wds-icon-tiny section-edit-pencil-icon"><use xlink:href="#wds-icons-pencil-tiny"></use></svg>edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>"Oliver" is from <i>Olivier</i>, a Norman French form of a Germanic name such as <i>Alfher</i> or an Old Norse name such as <i>√Åleifr</i>. The spelling was altered by association with Latin <i>oliva</i> "olive tree". In the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a> the name became well-known in Western Europe because of the French epic <i>La Chanson de Roland</i>, in which Olivier was a friend and advisor of the hero Roland.
</p><p>In England Oliver was a common medieval name, however it became rare after the 17th century because of the military commander Oliver Cromwell, who ruled the country following the civil war. The name was revived in the <a href="/wiki/19th_century" title="19th century">19th century</a>, perhaps in part due to the title character in <a href="/wiki/Charles_Dickens" title="Charles Dickens">Charles Dickens</a>' novel <i>Oliver Twist</i> (<a href="/wiki/1838" class="mw-redirect" title="1838">1838</a>).
</p>
