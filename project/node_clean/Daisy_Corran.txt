<p><b>Daisy Corran</b> was a <a href="/wiki/Ravenclaw" title="Ravenclaw">Ravenclaw</a> student who began attending <a href="/wiki/Hogwarts_School_of_Witchcraft_and_Wizardry" title="Hogwarts School of Witchcraft and Wizardry">Hogwarts School of Witchcraft and Wizardry</a> in <a href="/wiki/1990" title="1990">1990</a> or <a href="/wiki/1991" title="1991">1991</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-age_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-age-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> During the <a href="/wiki/1996%E2%80%931997_school_year" title="1996–1997 school year">1996–1997 school year</a>, she, <a href="/wiki/Banga_O%27Deluga" title="Banga O&#39;Deluga">Banga O'Deluga</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Justin_Steele" title="Justin Steele">Justin Steele</a> gave <a href="/wiki/Harry_Potter" title="Harry Potter">Harry Potter</a> two <a href="/wiki/Beater%27s_bat" title="Beater&#39;s bat">Beater's bats</a> in exchange for six <a href="/wiki/Gobstones" title="Gobstones">Gobstones</a> while in the <a href="/wiki/The_Quad" title="The Quad">Paved Courtyard</a>. Later that same term, she could be found in the <a href="/wiki/Seventh-floor_corridor" title="Seventh-floor corridor">seventh floor corridor</a>, where she traded <a href="/wiki/Potion" title="Potion">potions</a> for <a href="/wiki/Wizard%27s_Chess" title="Wizard&#39;s Chess">chess</a> pieces.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/wiki/Daisy_Corran?veaction=edit&amp;section=1" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/wiki/Daisy_Corran?action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology"><svg class="wds-icon wds-icon-tiny section-edit-pencil-icon"><use xlink:href="#wds-icons-pencil-tiny"></use></svg>edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The name "Daisy" is from the English word for the white flower, ultimately derived from Old English <i>dægeseage</i> meaning "day eye". It was first used as a given name in the 19th century, at the same time many other plant and flower names were coined.
</p>
