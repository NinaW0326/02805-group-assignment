<p><b>Mr Mulpepper</b> was a <a href="/wiki/Wizardkind" title="Wizardkind">wizard</a> and <a href="/wiki/Potioneer" title="Potioneer">potioneer</a> who owned and ran <a href="/wiki/Mr_Mulpepper%27s_Apothecary" title="Mr Mulpepper&#39;s Apothecary">Mr Mulpepper's Apothecary</a>. In <a href="/wiki/1992" title="1992">1992</a>, he sold <a href="/wiki/Harry_Potter" title="Harry Potter">Harry Potter</a> a potion <a href="/wiki/Phial" title="Phial">vial</a> for eight <a href="/wiki/Sickle" title="Sickle">Sickles</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> He also sold Harry and <a href="/wiki/Ginevra_Weasley" title="Ginevra Weasley">Ginny Weasley</a> two potion kits for the price of five <a href="/wiki/Wizarding_currency" title="Wizarding currency">sickles</a> each.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup>
</p>

<p class="mw-empty-elt"></p><figure class="thumb tleft show-info-icon" style="width: 132px"> 	<a href="https://static.wikia.nocookie.net/harrypotter/images/a/a9/Screenshot_1065.png/revision/latest?cb=20150430172935" class="image"><img alt="Screenshot 1065.png" src="https://static.wikia.nocookie.net/harrypotter/images/a/a9/Screenshot_1065.png/revision/latest/scale-to-width-down/130?cb=20150430172935" decoding="async" width="130" height="165" class="thumbimage" data-image-name="Screenshot 1065.png" data-image-key="Screenshot_1065.png" /></a> 	 	<figcaption class="thumbcaption"> 		 			<a href="/wiki/File:Screenshot_1065.png" class="info-icon"><svg><use xlink:href="#wds-icons-info-small"></use></svg></a> 		 		 		 	</figcaption> </figure>
<p class="mw-empty-elt"></p>
<ul><li>'Mulpepper' seems to be a reference to <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nicholas_Culpeper" class="extiw" title="wikipedia:Nicholas Culpeper">Nicholas Culpeper</a>, an English botanist and pharmacologist who was accused of witchcraft after angering his fellow physicians by condemning their greed and their use of harmful practises such as toxic remedies and bloodletting. A revolutionary, Culpeper was able to provide his services for free. This, and a willingness to examine patients in person rather than simply examining their urine created an affray between him and the Society of Apothecaries, who were similarly incensed by the fact that he suggested cheap herbal remedies as opposed to their expensive concoctions.</li></ul>
