<p><b>Jim McGuffin</b> was a <a href="/wiki/Non-magic_people" title="Non-magic people">Muggle</a> <a href="/wiki/Television" title="Television">television</a> weatherman in <a href="/wiki/1981" title="1981">1981</a>, working alongside <a href="/wiki/Ted" title="Ted">Ted</a>. McGuffin had predicted <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">rain</a> for <a href="/wiki/1_November" title="1 November">November 1st</a>, but due to the downfall of <a href="/wiki/Tom_Riddle" title="Tom Riddle">Lord Voldemort</a> and celebrations by the <a href="/wiki/Wizarding_world" title="Wizarding world">Wizarding community</a>, his forecasted rain became shooting stars across some areas of <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a>. Obviously unaware of the true reason, McGuffin wondered on air if people had been <a href="/wiki/Fall_of_Lord_Voldemort_celebrations" title="Fall of Lord Voldemort celebrations">celebrating</a> <a href="/wiki/Bonfire_Night" title="Bonfire Night">Bonfire Night</a> early. <a href="/wiki/Vernon_Dursley" title="Vernon Dursley">Vernon Dursley</a> watched the broadcast, prompting him to ask his wife, <a href="/wiki/Petunia_Dursley" title="Petunia Dursley">Petunia</a>, if the peculiar weather had anything to do with her <a href="/wiki/Lily_J._Potter" title="Lily J. Potter">sister's</a> "<a href="/wiki/Wizardkind" title="Wizardkind">people</a>."<sup id="cite_ref-PS1_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-PS1-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Behind_the_scenes">Behind the scenes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/wiki/Jim_McGuffin?veaction=edit&amp;section=1" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor" title="Edit section: Behind the scenes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/wiki/Jim_McGuffin?action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Behind the scenes"><svg class="wds-icon wds-icon-tiny section-edit-pencil-icon"><use xlink:href="#wds-icons-pencil-tiny"></use></svg>edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul><li>In fiction, a <b>MacGuffin</b> (sometimes <b>McGuffin</b> or <b>maguffin</b>) is a plot device in the form of some goal, desired object, or other motivator that the protagonist pursues, often with little or no narrative explanation. The specific nature of a MacGuffin is typically unimportant to the overall plot. The most common type of MacGuffin is a person, place, or thing (such as money or an object of value). Other more abstract types include victory, glory, survival, power, love, or some unexplained driving force.<sup id="cite_ref-Wikipedia_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wikipedia-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup></li></ul>
