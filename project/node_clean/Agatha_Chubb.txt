<b>Agatha Chubb</b> was a <a href="/wiki/Wizardkind" title="Wizardkind">witch</a> and an expert in ancient wizarding artefacts.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/wiki/Agatha_Chubb?veaction=edit&amp;section=1" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/wiki/Agatha_Chubb?action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography"><svg class="wds-icon wds-icon-tiny section-edit-pencil-icon"><use xlink:href="#wds-icons-pencil-tiny"></use></svg>edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>While researching <a href="/wiki/Quidditch" title="Quidditch">Quidditch</a> practises of the <a href="/wiki/16th_century" title="16th century">16th century</a>, she recovered no fewer than twelve lead <a href="/wiki/Bludger" title="Bludger">Bludgers</a> from marshes and peat bogs in <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">Ireland</a>. She used this discovery to demonstrate how modern <a href="/wiki/Wizardkind" title="Wizardkind">wizards</a> developed Bludgers from lead and then iron, as lead was determined too soft a metal for the purpose of the balls.<sup id="cite_ref-QTA6_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-QTA6-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>She wrote, <i>"They are undoubtedly Bludgers rather than cannon balls. The faint indentations of magically reinforced <a href="/wiki/Beater" title="Beater">Beaters</a>' <a href="/wiki/Beater%27s_bat" title="Beater&#39;s bat">bats</a> are visible and one can see the distinctive hallmarks of manufacture by a wizard (as opposed to a <a href="/wiki/Non-magic_people" title="Non-magic people">Muggle</a>) - the smoothness of line, the perfect symmetry. A final clue was the fact that each and every one of them whizzed around my study and attempted to knock me to the floor when released from its case."</i><sup id="cite_ref-QTA6_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-QTA6-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">Etymology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/wiki/Agatha_Chubb?veaction=edit&amp;section=2" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor" title="Edit section: Etymology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/wiki/Agatha_Chubb?action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Etymology"><svg class="wds-icon wds-icon-tiny section-edit-pencil-icon"><use xlink:href="#wds-icons-pencil-tiny"></use></svg>edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>"Agatha" is a Latinized form of the Greek name Αγαθη (<i>Agathe</i>), derived from Greek αγαθος (<i>agathos</i>) meaning "good". Saint Agatha was a 3rd-century martyr from Sicily who was tortured and killed after spurning the advances of a Roman official. The saint was widely revered in the Middle Ages, and her name has been used throughout Christian Europe (in various spellings). The mystery writer Agatha Christie (1890–1976) was a famous modern bearer of this name.
</p>
